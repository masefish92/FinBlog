I'm a CPA who works in tech. The Margin is where I write about corporate finance, financial statements, and individual companies — in the plain language I wish more finance writing used.

Why this exists

Most financial writing is built to make you feel like you need a credential to participate. I don't think that's true, and I don't think it's an accident, either — a reader who feels lost is a reader who pays someone else to explain things back to them. The Margin is my attempt at the opposite: show the mechanism, name the trick, hand over the vocabulary, and get out of the way.

What I cover

Three things, deliberately narrow in scope:

  • A glossary of finance and investment-banking terms, written so the definition and the "why it matters" are never more than a sentence apart.
  • Guides that walk through financial statements line by line, using one worked example instead of abstract theory.
  • Research on individual public companies — a real thesis, a real bull case, a real bear case, argued honestly rather than talked into a foregone conclusion.

Credentials, honestly

I'm a Certified Public Accountant. I'm not a registered investment advisor, and nothing on this site is personalized financial advice — see the disclaimer for the specifics. I write pseudonymously because I work full-time in the tech industry and this site is a separate, independent project; the accounting background is real, the byline is a pen name.

How I work

Every company breakdown starts from the primary filing — the actual 10-K, not a secondhand summary of one. Figures are rounded and clearly marked as approximate where I haven't independently re-verified them against the latest filing. The full methodology, including how corrections get handled, is on the editorial standards page.
