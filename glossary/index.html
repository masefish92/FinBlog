106 terms and counting

The glossary.

Every entry pairs a plain definition with the exact phrasing a vignette uses to signal it — because recognizing the cue is half the battle.

106 terms
A

Accrual accounting

Recording revenue and expenses when they're earned or incurred, not when cash actually changes hands.

Why it mattersAccrual accounting is exactly why net income and cash flow can diverge — and why sophisticated readers check both statements, not one.
Statements

Acquisition

One company buying enough of another to take control of it — one company 'swallowing' another, in plain terms.

Why it mattersThe buyer is called the 'acquirer,' the company being bought is the 'target' — those two labels show up constantly once a deal is announced.
M&A

Alternative investment

Any investment that isn't a plain stock, bond, or cash — hedge funds, private equity, real estate, commodities, and similar vehicles.

Why it mattersThese are usually harder and slower to sell than a stock, and charge higher fees, in exchange for returns that don't move in lockstep with the stock market.
Portfolio

Amortization

Like depreciation, but for intangible assets — patents, acquired software, capitalized loan costs — spread over their useful life.

Why it mattersWhen you see 'D&A' bundled together on a statement, amortization is usually the intangible half, often tied to a past acquisition.
Statements

Analyst

The most junior full-time role at a bank, typically filled by recent graduates, spent building models and slides for the deals senior bankers run.

Why it mattersIn Equity Research specifically, 'analyst' can instead mean a senior person whose name is on the published report — context tells you which one is meant.
Careers

Assets

Everything of value a person or company owns or is owed — cash, buildings, inventory, and money customers still owe them.

Why it mattersOn a balance sheet, assets always equal liabilities plus equity — not a coincidence, that's the entire reason the balance sheet balances.
Fundamentals
B

Basis point

One hundredth of a percentage point (0.01%) — the standard unit for describing small changes in interest rates or yields.

Why it matters'25 basis points' means 0.25%. Vignettes use bps instead of percent specifically to test whether you can convert correctly under time pressure.
Macro

Bear

An investor who believes prices are about to fall. A 'bear market' is a sustained slide, typically a 20%+ drop from a recent high.

Why it mattersBeing 'bearish' on one stock just means you expect that stock to fall — it doesn't necessarily mean you expect the whole market to.
Markets

Beta

A measure of how much a stock's price tends to move relative to the overall market — 1.0 moves with the market, above 1.0 amplifies it.

Why it mattersBeta measures historical co-movement, not risk of permanent loss — a low-beta stock can still be a terrible, overpriced business.
Portfolio

Bid/ask

Two prices quoted at once for the same security: the 'bid' is the highest price a buyer will pay, the 'ask' is the lowest price a seller will accept.

Why it mattersThe gap between the two — the bid-ask spread — is a quick, practical read on liquidity: narrow means an easy, cheap trade; wide means the opposite.
Trading

Block trade

A single trade involving a large chunk of stock (roughly 10,000+ shares) or bonds (roughly $200,000+), big enough to usually be arranged privately.

Why it mattersBlock trades get handled off the open market on purpose — dumping that much stock in all at once would tank the price before the trade finished.
Trading

Bond

A loan you make to a company or government: you hand over cash now, and they promise to repay it later (at 'maturity') plus interest along the way.

Why it mattersTreat a bond as an IOU with a schedule attached — the 'coupon' is the interest, 'maturity' is the repayment date.
Fixed Income

Bond yield

The annual return an investor earns holding a bond, accounting for price and coupon — moves inversely to bond price.

Why it mattersWhen a vignette says a bond's price fell, its yield rose, automatically — the two are mirror images of the same fact.
Fixed Income

Book value

Total assets minus total liabilities — shareholders' equity, as recorded on the balance sheet.

Why it mattersBook value understates the worth of asset-light, brand-driven businesses and can overstate the worth of firms full of aging physical assets.
Valuation

Bull

An investor who believes prices are about to rise. A 'bull market' is a sustained climb, usually driven by optimism about growth or earnings.

Why it mattersBull and bear are opposites and easy to mix up under time pressure — worth over-learning which is which before it matters.
Markets

Burn rate

The rate at which a company is spending cash faster than it earns it, usually measured monthly.

Why it mattersA 'high burn, high growth' story only works as long as growth keeps outrunning burn. When growth slows, burn becomes the headline.
Startups
C

Capital

Money or assets available to invest, spend, or use to generate more wealth — the fuel that keeps a business running and growing.

Why it matters'Raising capital' just means getting money from somewhere — selling stock, taking a loan, or bringing in outside investors.
Fundamentals

Capital expenditure

Cash spent on long-term physical assets — factories, machines, servers — that will be used for more than one year.

Why it matters'Capex-heavy' businesses (semiconductors, telecom) need much higher revenue growth to generate the same free cash flow as asset-light ones.
Statements

Capital markets

The marketplaces where companies and governments raise money by selling stocks and bonds to investors, and where those securities then keep trading.

Why it mattersIt's the umbrella term: Equity Capital Markets (ECM) handles stock deals, Debt Capital Markets (DCM) handles bond deals.
Fundamentals

Central bank

A country's official money authority — it sets interest rates, manages the money supply, and tries to keep inflation under control. The U.S. Federal Reserve is one example.

Why it mattersWhen a central bank raises rates, borrowing gets more expensive across the whole economy — usually the single biggest lever moving markets at once.
Macro

Chinese wall

An information barrier inside a bank that keeps the public-facing side (like Sales & Trading) from seeing confidential deal information sitting with the private side (like Investment Banking).

Why it mattersIt exists to prevent insider trading — a banker working on a secret merger shouldn't be able to tip off the trading desk before it's announced.
Careers

Commodities

Raw, physical materials — oil, gold, wheat, copper — bought and sold on their own exchanges, usually through standardized contracts.

Why it mattersCommodity prices move on real-world supply and demand (a drought, a mine closing) more than on any single company's news.
Markets

Common stock

The standard type of company share investors own — usually with voting rights and a claim on whatever's left after everyone else (lenders, preferred shareholders) gets paid.

Why it matters'Common' is the ordinary, default kind of stock, as opposed to preferred stock, which gets paid first but usually carries no vote.
Equity

Compounding

Earning returns not just on your original money, but on the returns that money already earned — interest on interest, growth on growth.

Why it mattersThe gap between simple growth and compounding is small in year one and enormous by year thirty — which is why starting early beats almost any other single decision.
Fundamentals

Controllers

The team inside a bank or company that makes sure the books are accurate — reconciling accounts, checking transactions, keeping the numbers honest.

Why it mattersIf traders and bankers are the ones making the money move, controllers are the ones making sure it gets recorded correctly afterward.
Careers

Convertible bond

A bond that can be swapped for a fixed number of shares later on, at the bondholder's choice — a loan with the option to turn into stock.

Why it mattersCompanies like issuing these because the interest rate is usually lower than a regular bond — investors accept less interest for the upside if the stock takes off.
Fixed Income

Coupon

The fixed periodic interest payment a bond pays its holder, usually stated as a percentage of face value.

Why it mattersThe coupon is fixed at issuance; it's the bond's price — not the coupon — that moves as market interest rates change.
Fixed Income

Covenant

A condition written into a loan or bond agreement that the borrower must maintain — a minimum ratio, a maximum debt level, and so on.

Why it mattersBreaching a covenant can trigger default even if the borrower is current on payments — it's a tripwire, not just a suggestion.
Credit

Credit rating

A letter grade (AAA down to D) from agencies like Moody's or S&P estimating the likelihood a borrower repays its debt in full and on time.

Why it mattersA downgrade doesn't just signal risk — it mechanically raises future borrowing costs and can trigger covenants in existing debt.
Credit

Current ratio

Current assets divided by current liabilities — a rough gauge of whether a company can cover its near-term obligations.

Why it mattersBelow 1.0 isn't automatically distress (some retailers run lean on purpose), but combined with falling cash it's worth a second look.
Ratios
D

Debt Capital Markets (DCM)

The bank team that helps companies and governments raise money by issuing bonds instead of stock.

Why it mattersIf a company wants to borrow hundreds of millions from public investors rather than take a bank loan, DCM structures and sells that bond deal.
Deal Teams

Debt-to-equity ratio

Total debt divided by shareholders' equity — how much of the company is financed by borrowing versus owner capital.

Why it mattersIndustry context matters enormously here — a utility at 1.5x is normal; a software company at 1.5x is a flashing warning light.
Credit

Depreciation

Spreading the cost of a physical asset (equipment, buildings) over its useful life instead of expensing it all at once.

Why it mattersDepreciation is a non-cash expense — it reduces net income but not cash. That's exactly why it gets added back in the cash flow statement.
Statements

Derivative

A financial contract whose value is derived from an underlying asset — a stock, bond, currency, or index — rather than having value on its own.

Why it mattersThe word alone signals leverage is likely in play: small price moves in the underlying can mean large moves in the derivative's value.
Derivatives

Dilution

Your ownership percentage shrinking because new shares get issued — stock options, secondary offerings, convertible notes converting.

Why it mattersWatch 'weighted average diluted shares' outstanding. If it's rising faster than net income, EPS growth is partly an illusion.
Equity

Discounted cash flow (DCF)

A valuation method that estimates a company's worth as the present value of all its projected future cash flows.

Why it mattersDCF outputs are extremely sensitive to the discount rate and terminal growth assumptions — small tweaks swing the valuation enormously.
Valuation

Dividend

A cash payment a company makes to shareholders out of its profits, usually on a regular schedule.

Why it mattersA dividend cut is one of the strongest negative signals in markets — management would rather do almost anything else first.
Equity

Due diligence

The homework done before a deal closes — digging through a company's financials, contracts, and records to confirm it's really what it claims to be.

Why it matters'DD' is where deals go to die — a skeleton found during due diligence (hidden debt, a lawsuit, cooked books) can sink an acquisition at the last minute.
M&A

Duration

A measure of a bond's price sensitivity to interest rate changes, expressed in years.

Why it mattersLonger duration means more pain when rates rise. A vignette stressing 'long-dated bonds' is setting up a rate-sensitivity question.
Fixed Income
E

EBITDA

Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization — an attempt to show operating profit stripped of financing and accounting choices.

Why it mattersManagement loves EBITDA because it hides debt load and capital intensity. Always ask what it's hiding before you use it to compare companies.
Valuation

Enterprise value

Market cap plus total debt minus cash — what it would actually cost to buy the whole company, debt included.

Why it mattersEnterprise value is the number you use to compare companies with different capital structures on an apples-to-apples basis.
Valuation

Equity

Ownership. In a company, it's the value left for shareholders after all debts are paid; in a house, it's what you actually own outright rather than what the bank owns.

Why it matters'Equity' and 'stock' are often used interchangeably when people talk about owning part of a public company.
Fundamentals

Equity Capital Markets (ECM)

The bank team that helps companies raise money by selling stock, including running IPOs and follow-on share sales.

Why it mattersECM is the stock-side counterpart to DCM's bond-side work — together they cover most of how companies raise outside money.
Deal Teams

Equity research

The team that studies companies and industries in depth and publishes opinions on whether a stock is worth buying — the people writing the 'buy/sell/hold' calls.

Why it mattersEquity research is meant to sit on the public side of the Chinese wall — its opinions are supposed to stay independent of whatever the bank's dealmakers are working on.
Careers

Executive Director (ED)

A mid-to-senior banking title, one rung below Managing Director — an ED runs deal teams day-to-day and reports up to an MD.

Why it mattersBank titles typically run, junior to senior: Analyst → Associate → Vice President → Executive Director → Managing Director.
Careers
F

Financial Sponsors Group (FSG)

A bank team that specializes in working with private equity firms and other investment funds as clients, rather than regular operating companies.

Why it mattersIf a bank's client is itself an investment firm buying and selling companies, FSG is often the team covering that relationship.
Deal Teams

Fixed income

Investments — mainly bonds — that pay a set, predictable interest rate on a schedule, instead of a return that depends on how well a company performs.

Why it matters'Fixed income' is often just shorthand for 'the bond market' as a whole, as opposed to 'equities' meaning the stock market.
Fixed Income

Float

Premiums an insurer has collected but not yet paid out in claims — cash it holds and can invest in the meantime.

Why it mattersBerkshire Hathaway's entire investment engine is built on float. 'Underwriting profit' in a vignette means the float was essentially free money.
Insurance

Flotation

Taking a company public by selling its shares to outside investors for the first time — another word for an IPO, more common in British English.

Why it mattersIf a company is described as planning to 'float,' this is the meaning — not to be confused with insurance float, a completely different concept.
M&A

Forward contract

A private agreement between two parties to buy or sell something at a set price on a future date, with terms negotiated to fit whatever they need.

Why it mattersForwards are the customizable, over-the-counter cousin of futures — futures are standardized and exchange-traded, forwards are one-off private deals.
Derivatives

Free cash flow

Cash from operations minus capital expenditures — the cash a business actually generates after reinvesting in itself.

Why it mattersA company can report positive net income and negative free cash flow at the same time. When they diverge for multiple years, trust cash flow.
Statements

Futures contract

A standardized agreement, traded on an exchange, to buy or sell an asset at a set price on a specific future date.

Why it mattersBecause futures are standardized and exchange-traded, they're easy to buy and sell again well before the delivery date arrives — the key difference from a forward.
Derivatives

FX

Shorthand for 'foreign exchange' — the market where one country's currency gets traded for another's.

Why it mattersFX is the largest, most liquid market in the world by trading volume — currencies trade nearly around the clock, five days a week.
Macro
G

Global Capital Markets (GCM)

The umbrella division at a bank that houses both the stock-focused (ECM) and bond-focused (DCM) fundraising teams.

Why it mattersThink of GCM as the parent group, with ECM and DCM as its two main departments underneath.
Deal Teams

Goodwill

The premium an acquirer pays above the fair value of a target's identifiable net assets, recorded as an intangible asset.

Why it mattersLarge goodwill balances are a bet that never gets marked to market until it fails — watch for 'impairment' language in the footnotes.
M&A

Gross margin

Revenue minus cost of goods sold, divided by revenue — what's left after paying for the stuff you sold, before overhead.

Why it mattersA high, stable gross margin over time is often the first clue of pricing power. A margin that's compressing quarter over quarter is a cost or competition story.
Ratios
H

Hedge

A position taken specifically to offset potential losses in another position — insurance against an adverse price move.

Why it mattersA 'perfect hedge' fully offsets risk but also fully offsets upside. Most real hedges are partial — ask what risk is left uncovered.
Derivatives

Hedge fund

A privately managed investment fund with fewer restrictions than a typical mutual fund — it can bet on prices falling (short selling) and borrow to amplify its bets (leverage).

Why it mattersHedge funds chase 'absolute return' — making money regardless of whether the overall market is up or down — rather than just trying to beat a benchmark.
Portfolio
I

Impairment

A write-down of an asset's book value when its recoverable value falls below what's carried on the balance sheet.

Why it mattersImpairments are non-cash but very real admissions that a past acquisition or investment isn't worth what was originally paid for it.
Statements

Inflation

The rate at which the general price level for goods and services rises, eroding the purchasing power of money over time.

Why it mattersRising inflation typically pressures central banks to raise interest rates, which in turn pressures bond prices and equity valuations.
Macro

Interest coverage ratio

Operating profit (EBIT) divided by interest expense — how many times over a company can pay the interest on its debt.

Why it mattersA ratio drifting toward 1.0x means nearly all operating profit is going to lenders before shareholders see a cent — a classic distress precursor.
Credit

IPO

Short for 'initial public offering' — the first time a company sells shares to the general public and lists on a stock exchange.

Why it mattersBefore an IPO, a company is 'private' and its shares aren't easy for ordinary investors to buy — the IPO is the moment that changes.
M&A

Issue

A batch of new stocks or bonds released for sale by a company or government. 'Issuing' securities just means creating and selling them.

Why it mattersA 'new issue' is freshly created stock or debt — different from shares that already exist and are simply changing hands on the market.
Fundamentals
L

LBO

Short for 'leveraged buyout' — buying a company using mostly borrowed money, with the target's own future cash flow expected to pay off that debt over time.

Why it matters'Leveraged' is the tell: a big chunk of the purchase price is debt, not the buyer's own cash, which raises both the potential return and the risk.
M&A

Leveraged Acquisition Finance (LAF)

A bank team that arranges the borrowed money — loans and bonds — used to fund leveraged buyouts and other acquisitions.

Why it mattersIf a private equity firm needs to borrow billions to fund a buyout, LAF is often the team structuring that debt.
Deal Teams

Liquidity

How quickly and cheaply an asset can be converted to cash without materially moving its price.

Why it mattersIn a crisis, correlations rise and liquidity dries up simultaneously — the exact moment investors most need to sell is when it's hardest to do so.
Markets
M

M&A

Short for 'mergers and acquisitions' — both the team at a bank and the broader activity of companies buying, selling, and combining with one another.

Why it mattersM&A bankers advise on the deal itself — negotiating price and terms — while a team like LAF might separately arrange the debt financing behind it.
M&A

Managing Director (MD)

The most senior banker on a deal team — MDs bring in clients, set strategy, and carry ultimate responsibility for the relationship and the deal.

Why it mattersWhen someone says they 'made MD,' they mean they were promoted to the top rung of the banker career ladder.
Careers

Market capitalization

Share price multiplied by shares outstanding — the total value the market assigns to a company's equity.

Why it mattersMarket cap ignores debt entirely. Two companies with the same market cap can have wildly different total enterprise risk.
Valuation

Merger

Two companies combining to form one, at least on paper as relatively equal partners — unlike an acquisition, where one side clearly buys the other.

Why it mattersIn practice, most 'mergers of equals' still end up with one side's management and culture dominating — the 'equal' framing is often more diplomatic than literal.
M&A

Moat

A durable structural advantage — brand, network effect, cost position, switching costs, regulation — that protects a company's profits from competitors.

Why it matters'Wide moat' language in a vignette is a cue to assume pricing power and durability when modeling future cash flows.
Strategy
N

Net income

What's left after every expense, interest payment, and tax is subtracted from revenue. The 'bottom line.'

Why it mattersNet income can be flattered by one-time gains (selling a building, a tax credit) — check the footnotes before trusting a clean-looking number.
Statements
O

Operating leverage

The degree to which fixed costs (versus variable costs) amplify profit swings as revenue rises or falls.

Why it mattersHigh operating leverage means small revenue changes cause outsized profit changes — a double-edged sword in a downturn.
Operations

Option

A contract giving the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) an asset at a set price before a set date.

Why it matters'Right, not obligation' is the exact phrase that separates options from forwards and futures — vignettes test this distinction directly.
Derivatives
P

P/E ratio

Share price divided by earnings per share — how many dollars investors pay for one dollar of annual profit.

Why it mattersA low P/E isn't automatically 'cheap' — it can mean the market correctly expects earnings to fall. Always ask why the multiple is where it is.
Valuation

PEG ratio

The P/E ratio divided by expected earnings growth rate — an attempt to judge whether a high multiple is justified by growth.

Why it mattersA PEG near 1 is the textbook 'fairly valued' heuristic, but it leans hard on a growth forecast that's often just a guess.
Valuation

Plain vanilla

A financial product with no unusual or complicated features — the simplest, most standard version of whatever it is.

Why it mattersSeeing 'plain vanilla' in front of a product name (a plain vanilla bond, a plain vanilla option) is a signal this is the basic version, not some exotic variant.
Derivatives

Principal

The original sum of money invested or borrowed, before any interest or returns are added on top.

Why it matters'Paying down principal' on a loan means reducing what you actually owe — separate from the interest charged on it, even though a single payment usually covers both at once.
Fundamentals

Private equity

Money invested directly into private companies — or companies taken private — with the goal of improving them and selling at a profit later.

Why it mattersPrivate equity firms typically hold a company for several years, actively involved in running it, before selling or taking it public again.
Portfolio

Private Wealth Management (PWM)

The part of a bank that manages money for wealthy individuals and families, rather than for institutions or companies.

Why it mattersPWM clients are people, not corporations — think financial planning and investment management for high-net-worth individuals.
Careers

Privatization

Turning a government-owned or public organization into a privately owned one — the opposite direction from an IPO.

Why it mattersPrivatization is a political decision as much as a financial one — it's often controversial because it hands public control of something (a utility, a transit system) to private owners.
M&A

Proprietary trader

A trader who buys and sells using the bank's own money to generate a direct profit for the firm, rather than trading on behalf of a client.

Why it mattersThis differs from a normal trading desk that executes trades for clients and earns a commission — a 'prop trader' is betting with the house's own money.
Careers

Public Information Book (PIB)

A compiled collection of everything publicly available about a company — filings, news, analyst reports — assembled for a deal team before they start working on that company.

Why it mattersA PIB is usually one of the first things put together at the start of a deal — the shared starting point everyone on the team reads first.
Deal Teams
Q

Quick ratio

Like the current ratio, but excludes inventory — a stricter test of whether near-term bills can be paid without selling stock.

Why it mattersUseful for spotting companies whose 'healthy' current ratio is really just a pile of inventory that may not sell quickly.
Ratios
R

Recapitalization

Reshuffling a company's mix of debt and equity — for example, swapping some debt for stock — without necessarily changing the underlying business.

Why it mattersRecapitalizations often happen when a company is in financial distress and lenders agree to convert some of what they're owed into ownership instead.
Credit

Research analyst

A senior member of an equity research team whose name and opinion appear on published reports about a stock or industry.

Why it mattersWhen financial news says 'analysts expect,' it's usually citing research analysts like these, from banks and research firms across the market.
Careers

Retained earnings

The cumulative profit a company has kept (not paid out as dividends) since it was founded, sitting on the balance sheet.

Why it mattersA shrinking retained earnings balance despite reported profits usually means large dividends or buybacks are returning more than net income.
Statements

Return on equity (ROE)

Net income divided by shareholders' equity — how efficiently a company turns owners' capital into profit.

Why it mattersHigh ROE driven by heavy debt (leverage) is a different, riskier animal than high ROE driven by genuinely superior operations. Check the debt load.
Ratios

Return on invested capital (ROIC)

After-tax operating profit divided by total invested capital (debt plus equity) — profitability judged against all capital employed, not just equity.

Why it mattersROIC consistently above the cost of capital is one of the cleanest quantitative signs of a real competitive advantage.
Ratios

Revenue

The total money a company brings in from selling goods or services, before any costs are subtracted. Also called 'sales' or 'top line.'

Why it mattersWhen a vignette says 'top line growth,' it's talking about revenue, not profit — a company can grow revenue while losing more money than ever.
Statements

Risk

The chance that an investment loses money or doesn't perform the way you expected — every investment carries some amount of it.

Why it mattersRisk and reward are supposed to move together: if something offers a high return with suspiciously low risk, that's worth being skeptical of.
Fundamentals

Runway

How many months a company can keep paying its bills at the current cash burn rate before it runs out of money.

Why it mattersShrinking runway plus rising burn is the exact setup that forces a dilutive fundraise — watch for both moving in the wrong direction together.
Startups
S

Securities

The catch-all word for tradeable financial assets — mainly stocks and bonds, though the term covers other tradeable instruments too.

Why it matters'Security' is the generic umbrella word; 'stock' and 'bond' are the two specific types you'll actually encounter most often.
Fundamentals

Securitization

Bundling a large pool of loans — like thousands of mortgages — together and selling investors new securities backed by the cash flows from that pool.

Why it mattersThis is exactly the mechanism behind mortgage-backed securities — pooling individually risky loans into a package sold off in pieces.
Credit

Share buyback

A company repurchasing its own shares in the open market, reducing the share count outstanding.

Why it mattersBuybacks only create value if shares are repurchased below intrinsic value. Buying back overpriced stock destroys value just as surely as overpaying for an acquisition.
Equity

Spot market

A market where an asset is bought and sold for immediate delivery and payment — the 'spot price' is the price right now, not for some future date.

Why it matters'Spot' is the opposite of 'forward' or 'futures' — those lock in a price today for delivery later; spot is cash-and-carry, today.
Markets

Stock

A share of ownership in a company. Own one share of a company's stock, and you own a small slice of that business.

Why it matters'Stock' and 'shares' are used interchangeably in everyday conversation — 'I own some Apple stock' and 'I own some Apple shares' mean the same thing.
Equity

Syndicate

A group of banks that team up to jointly sell a large stock or bond offering, splitting up both the work and the risk.

Why it mattersDeals are often too big or too risky for one bank alone — spreading it across a syndicate means no single bank is stuck holding all the unsold securities.
Deal Teams
T

TMT

Shorthand for 'technology, media, and telecom' — a common way banks group together companies from these related industries.

Why it mattersTMT is one of the more common 'industry group' labels you'll see on a banker's team name or business card.
Deal Teams

Tombstone

A formal, often plain and text-heavy advertisement published in the financial press to announce a completed securities offering and list the banks involved.

Why it mattersTombstones are as much a marketing ritual as an announcement — a way for banks to publicly show off which deals they worked on.
Deal Teams

Trader

Someone whose job is buying and selling securities — for clients, for the firm, or both — reacting to price moves in real time.

Why it mattersA trader executes; an analyst researches. On a real desk the two roles work closely together but do genuinely different jobs.
Careers
V

Volatility

How much and how quickly an asset's price fluctuates over time, usually measured as standard deviation of returns.

Why it mattersVolatility is a measure of price movement, not of business quality — conflating the two is one of the most common beginner mistakes.
Portfolio
W

Weighted average cost of capital (WACC)

The blended cost of a company's debt and equity financing, weighted by how much of each it uses — the minimum return a project must clear.

Why it mattersWACC is the discount rate in most DCF models. A vignette lowering WACC without justification is quietly inflating a valuation.
Valuation

Working capital

Current assets minus current liabilities — the cash tied up (or freed up) running day-to-day operations.

Why it mattersReceivables growing faster than revenue is a working-capital red flag: customers are taking longer to pay, or sales are being pulled forward.
Operations
Y

Yield

The return an investment pays you, usually shown as a percentage of what you paid for it.

Why it mattersYield moves opposite to price for a fixed payment stream like a bond: pay less for the same coupon, and your yield goes up.
Fixed Income

Yield curve

A plot of bond yields across maturities, from short-term to long-term, for otherwise similar (usually government) bonds.

Why it mattersAn inverted curve — short-term yields above long-term — has preceded most U.S. recessions and is one of the most-watched macro signals.
