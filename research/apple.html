The thesis

Apple's moat was never really the iPhone. It's the cost of leaving the iPhone.

Every hardware sale drags a services subscription along behind it — App Store commissions, iCloud storage, Apple Music, AppleCare, and increasingly, advertising within Apple's own apps. Services revenue carries gross margins north of 70%, more than double the hardware segment's. As services grow as a share of the mix, blended gross margin rises almost mechanically, which is exactly what the chart above shows: margin climbing even in years when revenue growth stalled.

That's the quiet reframe worth sitting with: Apple increasingly behaves like a subscription business wearing a hardware company's headline numbers. The iPhone isn't the profit engine anymore — it's the customer acquisition cost.

The switching-cost moat is real and it compounds. iMessage lock-in, shared family plans, AirDrop, Handoff, a decade of purchased apps and photos tied to one ecosystem — each one is individually minor and collectively enormous. Pricing power shows up not as aggressive price hikes but as customers who simply don't shop around.

Capital return is the other half of the story: Apple has spent well over $600B on buybacks across the past decade, shrinking the share count every single year. Even flat net income can produce rising earnings per share when the denominator keeps getting smaller — worth remembering before crediting "growth" that's really just fewer shares.

Where reasonable people disagree

Bull case vs. bear case

Bull case

  • Services mix shift keeps pushing blended gross margin higher even without unit growth.
  • Installed base of 2B+ active devices is a recurring-revenue base most software companies would envy.
  • Buyback pace continues to compress share count, flattering per-share metrics for years to come.
  • Regulatory pressure on App Store fees is real but has moved slower, and mattered less, than bears expected.

Bear case

  • iPhone unit growth has been roughly flat for years — the hook itself is no longer growing.
  • China exposure cuts both ways: a large market that's also a geopolitical and competitive risk (local OEMs closing the gap).
  • Regulatory actions in the EU and US are chipping at App Store economics, the highest-margin part of services.
  • Valuation multiples leave little room for a mistake — this is priced as a compounder, not a hardware cyclical.
What to watch next
01

Services revenue as a percentage of total revenue — the clearest single number for tracking the mix shift thesis.

02

Greater China revenue trend — the one geography where the growth story has genuinely wobbled.

03

Any regulatory ruling that forces alternative App Store payment rails — a direct hit to the highest-margin line.

Don't take our word for it

Pull the actual 10-K.

Apple's filings are free on SEC EDGAR. Search "AAPL 10-K," open the "Segment Information" footnote, and check the services-versus-products revenue split yourself — it's the single most useful number in the whole document.
