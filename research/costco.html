The thesis

A flat 12–13% gross margin isn't a weakness at Costco. It's a promise, kept for decades.

Costco famously caps markups on branded merchandise around 14–15% and roughly 15% on its own Kirkland Signature products — a self-imposed ceiling most retailers would consider leaving money on the table. That discipline is the entire strategy: by refusing to widen margins even when it could, Costco keeps prices low enough that renewing a membership feels obviously worth it, year after year.

The $1.50 hot dog-and-soda combo, unchanged in price since 1985, is the cultural shorthand for this — but the real mechanism is membership fee income. Look at operating income and you'll find it sits remarkably close to total membership fees collected. Merchandise sales essentially fund themselves; membership is where the actual profit lives.

That structure produces one of the highest-quality recurring revenue bases in retail: membership renewal rates run in the neighborhood of 90% in the U.S. and Canada, which means the bulk of profit each year is close to pre-committed before a single item is scanned.

The tension is valuation. Costco trades at a significant premium to typical retailers, priced more like a subscription business than a grocer — which is arguably correct, but leaves little room for error. A membership fee increase lands well when execution is flawless; a stumble in renewal rates or a slowdown in new warehouse openings would be judged far more harshly than at a business priced for mediocrity.

Where reasonable people disagree

Bull case vs. bear case

Bull case

  • ~90% renewal rates create a profit base that's close to pre-committed each fiscal year.
  • Membership fee increases (periodic, historically well-tolerated) drop almost straight to operating income.
  • Low-margin pricing discipline is itself the moat — it's genuinely hard for competitors to match without matching the model.
  • International expansion (particularly Asia) remains an underpenetrated, multi-decade growth runway.

Bear case

  • Valuation multiple assumes flawless execution — there's little cushion if renewal rates or comps disappoint.
  • Merchandise margin is structurally capped by design, so profit growth leans almost entirely on membership and volume.
  • E-commerce economics are less naturally suited to Costco's bulk, warehouse-pickup model than to typical retail.
  • New warehouse growth is inherently capital-intensive and geographically finite in mature markets.

Don't take our word for it

Pull the actual 10-K.

Costco's filings are free on SEC EDGAR. Search "COST 10-K" and compare "Membership fees" against "Operating income" in the income statement — the relationship between those two lines is the whole thesis in one glance.
