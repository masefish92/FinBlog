The thesis

Nvidia doesn't sell chips. It sells the only chips that run the software everyone already built.

The hardware is genuinely excellent, but the durable part of the moat is CUDA — Nvidia's software layer that most AI research and production code has been written against for over a decade. Switching to a competitor's chip isn't just a hardware swap; it means rewriting and re-validating software stacks, which is exactly the kind of switching cost that protects pricing power even when a rival ships a technically comparable chip.

That's why gross margin jumped so sharply rather than drifting up gradually: demand for AI training and inference capacity outstripped supply so completely that Nvidia could price well above its historical range without losing customers who had no credible alternative on a useful timeline.

The concentration risk is the flip side of that same story. A small number of hyperscale customers — the largest cloud providers — account for an outsized share of revenue, and several of them are simultaneously funding their own custom AI chips specifically to reduce dependence on Nvidia. Customers who are also your best-funded potential competitors is an unusual position to be in.

Semiconductor demand has also always been cyclical. The current buildout could be the start of a durable new baseline of AI infrastructure spending, or it could be a capacity boom that overshoots and corrects — those two scenarios produce very different five-year revenue paths from the same starting point today.

Where reasonable people disagree

Bull case vs. bear case

Bull case

  • CUDA's software lock-in means even at-parity competing chips face a real adoption barrier.
  • AI infrastructure spending looks more like a durable capex cycle than a one-off boom, based on current guidance.
  • Full-stack expansion (networking, software, systems) raises the average deal size beyond just the chip.
  • Supply remains the binding constraint, not demand — a rare position for a hardware company to be in.

Bear case

  • Top customers are simultaneously Nvidia's biggest revenue source and its most motivated future competitors.
  • 73%+ gross margins invite competition and pricing pressure almost by definition — that's rarely a stable equilibrium.
  • Export restrictions on advanced chips to certain markets are a real, ongoing revenue risk, not a hypothetical one.
  • A slowdown in AI capex growth — not even a decline, just deceleration — could re-rate the stock sharply given current expectations.

What to watch next
01

Customer concentration disclosures — the percentage of revenue from the top handful of buyers, and whether it's rising or falling.

02

Gross margin trajectory as competitors ship viable alternatives — the first real test of CUDA's pricing power.

03

Capex guidance from the largest hyperscale customers — Nvidia's demand curve is effectively their spending plan.

Don't take our word for it

Pull the actual 10-K.

Nvidia's filings are free on SEC EDGAR. Search "NVDA 10-K" and open the "Concentration of Revenue" note — it names the customer concentration explicitly, in Nvidia's own words.
